The Story

A love that has to cross a war to come home

Coweta County, Georgia, 1941. Rose Vale has spent her whole life performing — a bright, bubbly belle whose dazzling chatter hides the watchful, exacting girl no one is allowed to meet. Two boys court her that last golden summer: Ray Dalton, gold and gregarious and certain the world will arrange itself around him, and quiet Jack Mercer, who never says much and sees everything — the one person alive who looks straight through her bright voice to the girl underneath.

When the war takes them both, Rose gives Jack the one truly precious thing she owns: half of her grandmother's jade pendant, an heirloom older and stranger than anyone in her family quite remembers. Wear it home to me, she tells him. Bring it back.

Then a telegram says missing. Then Ray comes home alone — a grieving hero with a story about how Jack died — and the stone he hands back to Rose begins, against everything she believes, to show her the truth: that Jack is alive, a world away, at the cold edge of another war. That the fall was no accident. And that the half-moon of jade at her throat is one piece of a sacred thing split a hundred years ago in a burning Indian village — a thing two peoples have hunted ever since, one in the name of power, one in the name of love.

To find the man everyone says is dead, Rose will weaponize the very mask that imprisoned her and cross a war no one will let a grieving girl cross. But the stone that is leading her home was never only hers to keep — and the hardest thing it will ask of her is not to hold on, but to let go.

The Cast

The Performer

A Georgia belle whose dazzling "bright voice" hides a watchful, forensic mind. Her victory is learning to set the mask down — and, when the war demands it, to wield it like a blade.

The One Who Sees

Quiet, literal, unhurried; he says almost nothing and notices everything. The single person alive who looks through Rose's performance to the girl underneath — and never looks away.

The Best Friend

☽

Devraj

The Keeper

The heir of a robbed South Indian hill village, hunting a hundred years for the stolen heart of his people's goddess — and the one who finally, against all faith, carries it home.

What It's About

To be seen is the only magic worth keeping

Being Seen

The deepest intimacy in the book is attention. To be truly seen — through the performance, through the war, through death itself — is the love that outlasts everything.

Love vs. Power

One prophecy, two readings: a sacred thing that joins many hearts can free a people by love, or enslave the world by force. The jade chooses love, and will answer nothing else.

The Letting-Go

The keeping was never the gift. The hardest, truest act of love is the open hand — giving back the most precious thing you own, because it was never yours to hold.

The Bright Voice

A mask can be a cage or a tool. Rose's whole arc is learning the difference — and what a woman does with the performance that kept her safe and kept her hidden.

For readers of The Nightingale (Kristin Hannah), The Winter Sea (Susanna Kearsley), Outlander (Diana Gabaldon), and the love-magic of Alice Hoffman.

The Geography

Two roads to one river

Jack is swept east into a second war; Rose crosses the world up the Persian Corridor to find the man everyone says is dead; and a stolen stone makes its hundred-year way home. Every road bends toward one cold green river in the Caucasus — the Karsai.

Chapter One

When I See Through You

The Two Shadows

There was a war on the far side of the world that summer, but it had not yet learned the road to Coweta County, and so on the Fourth of July in the year 1941 the whole county put on its second-best and came down to the church grove by the river to eat too much, court too openly, and pretend with its entire heart that the radio had been making the foreign parts up.

Rose Ashford Vale was sixteen years old and at the very top of her powers.

She knew it, too, which was half of what made it true. She moved through the grove the way a good swimmer moves through familiar water — easily, and faster than she appeared to, and with a perfect private knowledge of where the cold currents ran.

Two of the county's loops, that summer, bent toward Rose. Everybody had noticed. They had even, in the manner of counties, given the thing a name. The two shadows, they called the Mercer boy and the Dalton boy, because wherever Rose Vale's yellow dress went that summer, two young men went after it in their two different ways — the one out in front of her like sunshine, the other a step behind and to the side, quiet, where you did not see him until you turned your head.

⁂

When the big green firework went up — the best of the poor dozen, a great soft chrysanthemum of green fire that hung over the river — Rose did not watch the green fire. She turned her head, and found Jack Mercer at the dark edge of the quilts, lit green as a thing at the bottom of clear water, and found that he was not watching the fire either.

He was watching her. And he did not look away when she caught him.
